LLMs and Teaching

Prohibition, detection, and alternatives

The Situation in 2026

Three years after ChatGPT’s release, the evidence is clear: most university students use GenAI tools regularly, regardless of course policies. Surveys across multiple countries and institutions consistently find usage rates above 80%. A 2025 study at Middlebury College found that over 80% of students used GenAI for coursework — one of the fastest technology adoption rates ever documented in higher education. A UK survey the same year put the figure at 92% of undergraduates using AI in some aspect of academic work.

This does not mean every student is using GenAI to cheat. Most use it for legitimate tasks — explaining concepts, brainstorming, grammar checking. But it does mean that prohibition, as a policy, exists in tension with the reality of student behaviour. Faculty who choose prohibition need to understand both why it is worth attempting and why it is difficult to enforce, so they can design their courses accordingly.

This section is addressed to faculty in all positions on the spectrum: those who want to prohibit GenAI use, those who want to permit it cautiously, and those still deciding. The goal is practical advice grounded in what we have learned since 2023.

Why Prohibition Is Difficult

Prohibition is a legitimate pedagogical choice, particularly in courses designed to develop foundational writing and research skills. But it faces several structural challenges:

Ubiquitous access. GenAI tools are free, require only a web browser, and are increasingly integrated into the software students already use — word processors, search engines, email clients, and phones. There is no way to prevent access outside the classroom, and increasingly little way to prevent it inside the classroom.

Unreliable detection. AI detection tools (Turnitin’s AI detector, GPTZero, Originality.ai, and others) do not work reliably enough to serve as evidence of misconduct. Independent testing has found false positive rates far higher than vendors claim, and the tools are systematically biased against non-native English speakers and neurodivergent students. A growing number of universities — including Vanderbilt, Johns Hopkins, UCLA, the University of Waterloo, Michigan State, and the University of Pittsburgh — have disabled Turnitin’s AI detection feature entirely. Several students have successfully sued or settled after false accusations based on AI detector output.

Evasion tools. A thriving industry of “humanizer” tools (Undetectable.ai, StealthWriter, StealthGPT, and many others) rewrites AI-generated text to evade detection software. These tools are cheap or free, easy to use, and specifically marketed to students. Turnitin announced “AI bypasser detection” in August 2025, but this creates an arms race that detection is unlikely to win. The existence of these tools means that even if detection software were accurate — which it is not — determined students could circumvent it.

Chilling effect. Surveys find that roughly half of students report being afraid to use AI in any capacity for fear of false accusations, even in courses where some use is permitted. This punishes cautious, honest students while doing little to deter deliberate misuse.

The fairness problem. This is perhaps the most corrosive consequence of unenforced prohibition, and it deserves frank acknowledgement. When a significant number of students use GenAI to polish their writing while others do not, the overall quality of submissions rises. Faculty may not consciously curve grades, but they calibrate expectations against what they see. A student who writes competently but with occasional passive voice, awkward transitions, or poorly structured paragraphs — the normal imperfections of developing writers — will appear weaker relative to a cohort whose prose has been quietly smoothed by an LLM. The honest student’s B+ becomes a B; the AI-polished student’s B+ becomes an A-.

Students notice this. They see roommates and friends submit AI-assisted work and receive high marks. The message is clear: the system rewards those who use GenAI and punishes those who do not, regardless of what the syllabus says. This breeds cynicism, erodes trust in the instructor, and creates pressure on honest students to start using the tools themselves — not because they want to, but because they feel they have no choice.

This dynamic is one of the strongest arguments for moving beyond prohibition where possible, or at least for coupling prohibition with assessment methods (oral exams, in-class writing, process documentation) that cannot be gamed in this way. If the grade reflects demonstrated understanding rather than prose quality alone, the advantage of AI-polished text largely disappears.

None of this means prohibition is impossible. It means that prohibition enforced primarily through detection is a losing strategy, and that unenforced prohibition creates its own injustices. Effective prohibition requires a different approach.

The Problem with AI Detection Tools

This deserves its own section because faculty may be under the impression that detection tools are a viable safeguard. They are not — and at the University of Saskatchewan, no AI detection tool has been approved for use. The university’s Academic Integrity office explicitly warns that these tools are biased against non-native English writers, produce false positives and false negatives, and can be bypassed through simple techniques. It further advises that instructors should not submit student work to third-party detection tools without permission, as doing so may constitute copyright infringement. Students who believe their work has been submitted to an unapproved detection tool without consent are directed to report this to their Associate Dean.

This is not merely a matter of institutional policy. It means that AI detector output cannot serve as evidence in a misconduct proceeding at this university. Faculty who rely on detection tools to identify AI use are building on a foundation that will not hold up if challenged.

False Positives

AI detection tools work by measuring statistical features of text — perplexity (how surprising the word choices are) and burstiness (how much sentence length and structure vary). Human writing tends to be more variable; AI-generated text tends to be more uniform. But these are tendencies, not rules, and the overlap is large.

Independent research has documented:

  • False positive rates between 5% and 20% on general student submissions, far higher than the <1% vendors claim.
  • A Washington Post test that produced a 50% false positive rate on certain content using Turnitin’s detector.
  • Accuracy that drops to 80–85% when text has been lightly paraphrased or edited — which is how most students actually use GenAI.

Bias Against Non-Native English Speakers

This is the most serious problem. A widely cited 2023 study found that AI detectors misclassified over 61% of essays by non-native English speakers as AI-generated, while achieving near-perfect accuracy on native English speaker essays. A follow-up found that 97% of TOEFL essays were flagged by at least one detector.

The reason is structural: detectors penalize simpler vocabulary, shorter sentences, and more formulaic structures — precisely the features of competent second-language academic writing. This is not a bug that can be patched; it is inherent to how the technology works.

In 2025, multiple institutions reported cases of international students accused of AI cheating based on detector output that was later shown to be wrong. A 2025 study found that Black students were disproportionately accused, with 20% reporting false accusations compared to 7% of white students.

Bias Against Neurodivergent Students

Students on the autism spectrum often produce writing that is highly structured, literal, and consistent in tone — features that overlap with the statistical signatures detectors associate with AI. Students with ADHD who write in focused bursts can produce similar patterns. Documented cases include students falsely accused who had to disclose their diagnoses to clear their names.

The Institutional Response

The American Historical Association published its Guiding Principles for Artificial Intelligence in History Education in August 2025, reflecting a two-year process of consultation. The AHA emphasizes that GenAI “can produce material that appears polished and credible, but assessing their outputs demands critical skills that the models themselves can neither teach nor foster.” The framing is disciplinary rather than technological: the question is what historical training requires, not what detection tools can do.

A growing consensus among teaching and learning centres holds that detection-based enforcement is unsustainable. As Inside Higher Ed reported in December 2025: “You can’t AI-proof the classroom, experts say. Get creative instead.”

Best Practices for Prohibition

If you choose to prohibit GenAI use in your courses — and there are good reasons to do so, especially in first-year writing courses — the following practices are more effective than relying on detection software.

In-Class Writing

The most straightforward safeguard is supervised writing. If a significant assessment takes place in the classroom, GenAI cannot be used. Options include:

  • In-class essay exams (open-book or closed-book).
  • Supervised writing sessions where students work on drafts during class time.
  • In-class primary source analysis exercises.

Limitations: In-class writing tests speed as well as understanding and cannot be the sole assessment method for courses with substantial research components. There is also a serious accessibility constraint: students registered with Access and Equity Services (AES) often require extended time, separate spaces, or alternative formats for timed assessments. AES does not currently have the resources to support every course if large numbers of instructors simultaneously shift to 100% in-class assessment. A department-wide move toward in-class writing would place unsustainable demands on accessibility services and risk disadvantaging precisely the students these services exist to support. In-class writing is a useful component of a broader assessment strategy, but it cannot be the whole strategy.

Process-Based Assessment

Rather than evaluating only the final product, require evidence of the writing process:

  • Staged submissions. Require an annotated outline, a rough draft, and a revised draft at intervals across the term. Each stage should show genuine evolution — new ideas appearing, arguments being restructured, evidence being added or reconsidered.
  • Research logs. Have students maintain logs of sources consulted, search strategies used, and how their argument developed. These are difficult to fabricate convincingly because they require the kind of specific, idiosyncratic detail that GenAI does not produce well.
  • Reflective annotations. Ask students to annotate their own drafts, explaining why they made specific argumentative choices, where they struggled, and what they would change.

Limitations: Process-based assessment raises the bar for misuse, but it does not eliminate it. A student using GenAI can generate an outline, then a rough draft, then a polished revision, submitting each stage on schedule — producing process artefacts that look indistinguishable from genuine incremental work. Research logs can be fabricated after the fact. Reflective annotations can be generated by the same tool that produced the draft. The fundamental problem is that current models are good enough to simulate process, not just product: they can produce plausible false starts, evolving arguments, and self-aware reflections on writing choices.

This does not make process-based assessment pointless. It still catches the least sophisticated misuse (a student who submits a polished final essay with no prior stages), and the combined effort of generating convincing process artefacts and a final paper through GenAI is substantially greater than simply submitting an AI-generated essay. But instructors should be aware that staged submissions alone are not proof of genuine engagement. The strongest safeguard remains an oral component — a conversation in which the student must explain, in their own words, how their argument developed and why they made the choices they did. A student who fabricated the process will struggle to discuss it convincingly.

Version History as a Writing Process Record

Some word processors maintain a timestamped record of every addition, deletion, and edit made to a document. Google Docs is the best-known example, and version history tracking is widely used in K–12 and at other universities as a way to observe the writing process. The idea is straightforward: if you can see how a document was written, you can distinguish between text that was composed incrementally (with pauses, false starts, and revisions) and text that was pasted in as a finished block.

Google Docs Is Not Approved at USask

At the University of Saskatchewan, Google Docs is not on the approved learning technology list. Faculty should not require students to compose coursework in Google Docs. If you want to use version history tracking, use Microsoft 365 (Word Online), which is fully supported and integrated, or check whether Canvas provides relevant tracking for your assignment type. See the warning below for why this matters in misconduct proceedings.

What version history reveals:

  • Typing patterns. Genuine student writing shows incremental word-by-word and sentence-by-sentence additions, with pauses, deletions, false starts, and revisions.
  • Copy-paste events. AI-assisted work often appears as large blocks of text pasted in whole, with minimal subsequent editing. This is visible in the revision history.
  • Time spent. Total editing time and the number of sessions are logged. A 3,000-word essay that appears fully formed in a single 20-minute session is a different object than one built over five sessions across two weeks.
  • Editing trajectory. You can see whether ideas evolved organically or appeared fully formed.

Browser extensions like Revision History and Draftback enhance this further by providing video replay of the writing process.

Check Your Institution’s Approved Software

Before adopting any version-history approach, verify that the tools you plan to use are approved by your institution’s IT services. At many universities, Google Docs is not an approved platform for coursework, and third-party browser extensions like Revision History are almost certainly not vetted or supported.

This matters most in the context of academic integrity. If you use version history evidence to support a misconduct finding and the student challenges the decision, your case may be undermined if the evidence was gathered through unapproved software. IT policies exist in part to ensure that institutional processes — including disciplinary ones — rest on defensible foundations.

Faculty routinely use unapproved tools for their own convenience, and in most contexts the risk is low. But a misconduct proceeding is not most contexts. If you plan to use version history as part of your assessment or integrity strategy, confirm with your IT department that the platform is approved for student use, and ensure the tool’s data handling complies with your institution’s privacy policies.

At the University of Saskatchewan, for example, Google Docs is not on the approved learning technology list, while Microsoft 365 is fully supported and integrated. Word’s built-in version history offers similar functionality within the approved ecosystem. Canvas, USask’s LMS, may also provide relevant tracking depending on how assignments are configured.

Limitations even with approved tools: A determined student could type AI-generated text character by character, though this is time-consuming enough to be largely self-defeating — or it was, until recently. An ecosystem of tools now exists to automate exactly this. Often marketed as “Auto Typers,” “Ghost Typers,” or “Humanizers,” these tools simulate keystroke-by-keystroke typing into browser-based editors so that AI-generated text appears to have been composed in real time, complete with realistic pauses and corrections. Popular examples currently available in the Chrome Web Store include Duey.ai Auto Typer, Undetectable AI Human Typer, and SynthTyper, alongside various free, open-source scripts on GitHub. A student using one of these tools would produce a version history indistinguishable from genuine incremental composition.

This does not make version history worthless — most students will not go to this length, and the approach remains useful as one component of a broader assessment strategy. But it does mean that version history, like detection software, should not be treated as dispositive evidence of academic integrity. Students who draft in another application and paste will be visible in the history, which itself becomes a point of conversation.

Implementation: If you adopt this approach with an approved tool, state in your syllabus that all written work must be composed in the specified platform. Explain that you may review the version history. This is transparent, does not rely on detection software, and provides a basis for conversation rather than accusation.

Assignment Design That Resists GenAI

A common suggestion is to assign analysis of obscure or unpublished primary sources that are unlikely to be in the LLM’s training data. The logic is that the model cannot analyse what it has not seen. This was plausible in 2023 and 2024, but current models are multimodal: a student can photograph or upload any document directly into the chat, and the model will produce a competent analysis regardless of whether the source appeared in its training data. The obscurity of the source no longer provides meaningful protection.

That said, some assignment designs do raise the bar, even if none are fully AI-proof:

  • Personal engagement with sources. Ask students to reflect on their experience navigating a finding aid, visiting an archive, or encountering a physical document. “Describe what surprised you about the condition of the original manuscript” cannot be answered by a tool that has never been in the room. This is the most resistant assignment type, because it requires embodied experience rather than textual analysis.
  • Course-specific content. Require engagement with specific in-class discussions, guest lectures, or seminar debates. “How does Thompson’s argument about the moral economy relate to the point raised in seminar about the Saskatchewan wheat pools?” ties the assignment to a conversation the student had to be present for. A student could summarise the readings with GenAI, but cannot reconstruct the specific exchange that happened in class.
  • In-class source analysis. Rather than assigning primary source analysis as take-home work, conduct it in class under supervised conditions. This combines the pedagogical value of close reading with the assurance that the work is the student’s own.

Instructors should be realistic about what assignment design can and cannot accomplish. It can make outsourcing more effortful and less rewarding. It cannot prevent a determined student from using GenAI on any take-home assignment. The most reliable safeguard remains assessing understanding directly, whether through in-class work, conversation, or (at the upper level) oral examination.

Oral Verification

A short oral conversation about a written assignment is the single most effective safeguard against work a student does not understand. This can take several forms:

  • Oral exam on a research paper (15–20 minutes). The student discusses their argument, evidence, and research process. The grade reflects demonstrated understanding, not performance anxiety. One model already in use: “If you do the work yourself and write your paper, this will be a low-stress discussion of your evidence and arguments.”
  • Brief follow-up conversations (5–10 minutes). After returning a graded assignment, meet individually with students to discuss their work. This need not be framed as verification; it can be framed as feedback.
  • In-class presentations. Students present their research to the class and field questions. This is standard seminar pedagogy and serves double duty as GenAI verification.

Resource implications: Oral verification is time-intensive. A 15-minute conversation with each student in a class of 30 requires 7.5 hours of faculty time. This is feasible for seminars and upper-level courses but challenging for large lecture sections without TA support.

Moving Beyond Prohibition

Some faculty will conclude that prohibition, while legitimate, is not the right approach for their courses — particularly at the upper-undergraduate or graduate level. The following practices allow GenAI use while maintaining rigorous standards.

The Ownership Model

The most effective framework centres on ownership: students are intellectually responsible for every word they submit, regardless of how it was produced. GenAI may be used for specific tasks (literature review, transcription, copy editing, testing ideas), but the student must be able to explain, defend, and contextualize every claim in their work.

Under this model:

  • The syllabus specifies what GenAI use is permitted and what is not.
  • Students disclose any GenAI use.
  • An oral component (exam, defence, follow-up conversation) verifies understanding.
  • Work that the student cannot defend does not receive credit.
  • Students are advised that completing the core writing themselves is the safest approach.

This shifts the enforcement mechanism from “did you use AI?” (which cannot be reliably determined) to “do you understand your own work?” (which can be tested directly through conversation).

Disclosure Requirements

Where GenAI use is permitted, require disclosure. This normalizes transparency and makes concealment — rather than use — the integrity violation.

  • State in the syllabus exactly what must be disclosed and how.
  • Provide examples: “I used ChatGPT to identify relevant secondary literature, then verified each citation in JSTOR” or “I used Claude to help debug a Python script for data cleaning.”
  • Distinguish between incidental use (spell-check, grammar tools) and substantive use (drafting, research, analysis).

AI as Object of Study

One approach that serves both pedagogy and AI literacy: make GenAI output something students analyse critically rather than something they produce with.

  • Fact-checking exercises. Have students ask an LLM to summarize a historical debate, then identify what it gets wrong, what perspectives it omits, and what sources it fabricates. This teaches source criticism applied to a new kind of text.
  • Comparative analysis. Students write their own analysis of a primary source, then generate an LLM analysis of the same source, then write a critical comparison. Where does the LLM miss nuance? Where does it hallucinate context? What does it do well?
  • Historiographic audit. Ask an LLM to produce a reading list on a topic the student knows well. Have them evaluate it: which sources are real? Which are fabricated? Which are accurately characterized? This is a powerful exercise in bibliographic literacy.

These assignments cannot be completed by GenAI because the task is to evaluate GenAI’s limitations. They teach the critical skills students need whether or not they use these tools in their own research.

What We Know Works

After three years of institutional experimentation, a rough consensus is emerging:

  1. Detection tools are not the answer. They produce too many false positives, are biased against vulnerable student populations, and are defeated by freely available evasion tools. Multiple institutions have disabled them. Do not build your integrity strategy around detection.

  2. Assessment design is the answer. Assignments that require demonstrated understanding — through oral components, process documentation, in-class work, or engagement with specific and idiosyncratic sources — are more robust than any technological fix.

  3. Clarity is essential. Students report confusion about what is permitted. Every syllabus should state the course’s GenAI policy explicitly, including what is allowed, what is prohibited, and what must be disclosed.

  4. Instructor autonomy matters. Different courses have different goals. A first-year writing course and a fourth-year research seminar warrant different policies. Faculty should be supported in making the choice that serves their students and their pedagogy.

  5. The conversation is ongoing. No policy adopted today will be the right policy in three years. Build in review mechanisms and be willing to adjust.
