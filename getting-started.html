Getting Started

Your first session with a free tool

Choose a Tool

You can begin with either of these free options. Both require only an email address to create an account:

  • ChatGPT (chatgpt.com) — Made by OpenAI. The free tier gives you access to the GPT-4o model with some usage limits. This is probably the most widely used LLM.
  • Google Gemini (gemini.google.com) — Made by Google. Free with a Google account. Comparable capability to ChatGPT for most tasks.

Both tools work in a web browser. You type a message, the model responds, and you can continue the conversation. There is nothing to install.

For this guide, the examples use ChatGPT, but the prompts work in any tool with minor adjustments.

Your First Conversation

Open one of the tools above and try the following prompt. Copy and paste it, or adapt it to a topic you know well:

I am a historian studying the development of the western Canadian grain trade in the early twentieth century. Can you give me a brief overview of the major developments between 1900 and 1930, focusing on the role of grain elevators and the Winnipeg Grain Exchange?

Read the response. Some of it will be accurate. Some of it may be vague or slightly wrong. Notice:

  • Does it cite specific sources? (Probably not, or if it does, check whether they exist.)
  • Does it capture the broad outlines correctly?
  • Where does it feel generic — like a textbook summary rather than a historian’s analysis?

This is a useful calibration exercise. You now have a sense of what the tool knows and does not know about a topic you understand.

A More Useful Exercise: Transcription

LLMs are often more useful for concrete tasks than for open-ended questions. Try this:

  1. Find a page from a digitized historical document — a handwritten letter, a printed government report with difficult typography, or a census page. If you have a PDF on your computer, many LLM tools now allow you to upload images or documents directly.

  2. Upload or paste the text and ask:

Please transcribe the text in this image as accurately as possible. This is a [type of document] from [approximate date and context]. Preserve the original spelling and punctuation.

  1. Compare the output against the original. Where does it succeed? Where does it struggle?

For printed text in good condition, LLMs are often very accurate. For handwriting, the results are more variable but can still be a useful first pass that you then correct.

Understanding the Conversation Model

A few things are worth knowing about how these tools work in practice:

Context window. The model can only “remember” a certain amount of text within a single conversation. If your conversation becomes very long, the model may lose track of things you said earlier. For long tasks, it can help to start a new conversation and restate the key context.

System prompts and instructions. You can begin a conversation by telling the model what role you want it to play or what constraints to follow. For example:

You are helping me transcribe 19th-century French colonial correspondence. The documents are from the Archives nationales d’outre-mer. Please preserve the original French text and flag any words you are uncertain about with [?].

This kind of framing improves results significantly.

Follow-up questions. You can refine the model’s output by asking follow-up questions in the same conversation:

That transcription looks mostly right, but I think the word you read as “commerce” in line 3 is actually “commode.” Can you look at that section again?

The model will adjust. This iterative back-and-forth is often more productive than trying to get a perfect result on the first try.

What You Have Learned

After fifteen minutes with a free tool, you should have a sense of:

  • How the conversational interface works
  • What kind of output the model produces for topics you know
  • How transcription assistance works in practice
  • The importance of checking everything
