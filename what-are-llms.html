What Are Large Language Models?

A brief, non-technical orientation

The Short Version

Large language models (LLMs) are software systems trained on enormous quantities of text. When you type a prompt, the model generates a response by predicting what text is most likely to follow, drawing on patterns learned during training. The result often reads like it was written by a knowledgeable human — but the process behind it is fundamentally different from human thought.

Understanding this distinction matters. It explains both why these tools are useful and why they fail in characteristic ways.

How They Work (Without the Jargon)

Imagine you had read every book, article, and webpage published in English (and many other languages) up to a certain date. You would develop a powerful sense of what kinds of sentences follow other sentences — what arguments historians tend to make about the Atlantic slave trade, what a colonial office list looks like, how a French land grant is typically structured.

An LLM has something like that statistical sense, but without understanding. It does not know that the Treaty of Paris was signed in 1763 the way you know it. It has learned that in texts about the Seven Years’ War, the phrase “Treaty of Paris” is very often followed by “1763.” This is a crucial distinction — though as Cameron Blevins has argued, dismissing these tools as mere “autocomplete on steroids” is no longer tenable.1 The gap between what these models produce and what a knowledgeable human would write has narrowed considerably since 2024, and understanding how they work is essential for knowing when to trust them and when not to.

When the pattern is strong and well-represented in the training data, the model produces accurate, useful output. When the pattern is weak, ambiguous, or absent, the model can produce confident-sounding nonsense — and may not signal the difference.

What They Are Good At

LLMs excel at tasks where pattern recognition is the core skill:

  • Transcription assistance: Recognizing words in difficult handwriting or degraded print. The best current models now achieve accuracy comparable to expert human typists on English-language handwriting — a breakthrough that Mark Humphries has called “the bitter lesson” for the specialized HTR community.2
  • Translation: Producing serviceable rough translations, particularly for well-represented languages (French, Spanish, German, Latin).
  • Summarization: Condensing long texts into shorter summaries (useful for triage, not for close reading).
  • Code generation: Writing scripts in Python, R, or other languages for data tasks — often faster and more reliably than a historian learning to code from scratch.
  • Editing and proofreading: Catching typos, grammar errors, and inconsistencies in your drafted text.
  • Reformatting and structuring: Converting data between formats, extracting structured information from unstructured text.

Where They Struggle

LLMs have improved substantially since 2024, but they still fail in characteristic ways:

  • Factual accuracy: They hallucinate — generating plausible-sounding but false claims, including fabricated citations, invented dates, and non-existent sources. Hallucinations have become less frequent in the latest models, but this actually makes them more dangerous: when most output is accurate, it is easier to let your guard down and miss the one fabricated source that slips through.
  • Archival research: They cannot access unpublished sources, manuscript collections, or anything not in their training data. They have no way to visit an archive.
  • Interpretation and argument: Current models are surprisingly capable at synthesis and interpretation — they can identify historiographic debates, weigh competing claims, and construct plausible arguments. But the responsibility for evaluating whether an interpretation is sound, whether the evidence is sufficient, and whether the argument holds up still rests with the historian. The models are getting better at the form and substance of argument, but they do not know your sources the way you do.
  • Knowing what they do not know: They do not reliably flag uncertainty. A fabricated citation may be presented with the same confidence as a real one, though some models (notably Claude) are better than others at acknowledging gaps.

The Key Habit: Verify Everything

The single most important skill in using these tools is verification. Never trust an LLM’s output without checking it against a source you control. This applies especially to:

  • Citations and bibliographic references (check that the book or article actually exists)
  • Dates, names, and factual claims
  • Translations (compare against the original, or a second source)
  • Code (test it; does it actually do what you asked?)

If this sounds exhausting, it is worth noting that historians already verify sources as a matter of professional practice. The habit transfers directly. The difference is that with an LLM, you must verify the tool as well as the source.

A Note on Training Data

LLMs are trained on text that existed up to a certain date (the “knowledge cutoff”). They do not have access to the internet in real time unless specifically designed to search the web (some tools offer this as a feature). They reflect the biases, gaps, and emphases of their training data. For historians, this means:

  • They know more about topics that are well-represented in English-language digital text.
  • They know less about topics that are primarily documented in archives, rare books, or languages with less digital presence.
  • They can reproduce historiographic biases present in the literature they were trained on.

None of this is disqualifying, but it means you should treat LLM output the way you would treat any secondary source: as a starting point that requires critical evaluation, not as an authority.
